Think like which adversary?

"Think like an attacker" is bad advice without specificity. A financially motivated criminal, a nation-state espionage operator, and a hacktivist do not think the same way, want the same things, or respond to the same defences. Primer · Before you pick

What "adversary mindset" actually means

The phrase gets used loosely. The real skill underneath it is a disposition — not a list of techniques. Defenders inventory what they have; attackers ask what would let them succeed and find the cheapest path to it. The three habits that distinguish the disposition are below. Useful framing for any of the seven types you'll find further down.

01 Backwards from objectives

Attackers don't start from tools. They start from the outcome they need — money, intelligence, disruption, embarrassment — and work backwards to the cheapest path that produces it. Defensive thinking that starts from a control inventory misses paths attackers see immediately.

02 Cost calculus over capability

Most attackers will not use a sophisticated capability when a cheap one works. Defenders consistently overestimate the sophistication required to beat them. Looking for advanced TTPs while missing the off-the-shelf RMM tool is the canonical failure.

03 Comfort with partial knowledge

Attackers move with what they have. They don't wait for certainty. Defenders demand a complete picture before acting — a luxury attackers exploit. The asymmetry favours whoever tolerates ambiguity better; that is rarely the defender.
