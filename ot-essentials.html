OT Essentials · A practitioner's orientation

OT, for the IT-trained reader.

Operational Technology runs on different priorities, vocabulary, and assumptions than IT. The instincts that keep IT estates safe — patch fast, isolate fast, rotate fast — can cause real-world harm in OT. This is the orientation document for the practitioner who knows IT security well and is getting OT exposure for the first time, or working alongside OT engineers and needing to engage credibly.

Section 01

Why OT is different

Operational Technology controls physical processes — water treatment, electricity distribution, refineries, manufacturing lines, building HVAC, railway signalling. The job is to keep those processes running safely and predictably for years. The job is not to be patched on a monthly cadence, isolated when alerts fire, or rebooted to apply settings. That's the source of nearly every IT-OT misunderstanding worth knowing about.

The priority inversion

In IT, the unconscious priority order is some variant of confidentiality, integrity, availability. Most security decisions can be reasoned about with that ordering. In OT, the priority order inverts, and one extra priority sits above all of them: safety. The standard mnemonic is SAIC — Safety, Availability, Integrity, Confidentiality.

IT priority order

  1. Confidentiality — protect data from disclosure
  2. Integrity — protect data from modification
  3. Availability — keep services running

OT priority order

  1. Safety — protect people, environment, equipment
  2. Availability — keep the process running
  3. Integrity — protect process data and logic
  4. Confidentiality — protect data from disclosure

This isn't aesthetic. It changes what counts as a successful security control. An IT control that takes a system offline to prevent data exfiltration is doing its job. An OT control that takes a system offline to prevent the same thing has — depending on what the system controls — potentially caused a safety event.

The IT instinct trap

The instincts that make a competent IT security practitioner can produce unsafe actions in OT if applied without translation. Three particularly load-bearing ones:

"Patch it now"

In IT, "we'll patch next Tuesday" is acceptable for low-severity issues and unacceptable for high-severity ones. In OT, "we'll patch in the next planned outage window" is the normal answer regardless of severity, because patching a controller mid-process can crash it, change its behaviour subtly, or require revalidation work that takes weeks. Many controllers haven't been patched since they were installed — sometimes a decade ago — and they're working as intended. The control isn't "patch fast"; it's compensating controls and segmentation.

"Isolate it"

Isolation in IT means "pull it off the network so it can't spread or be reached." In OT, what looks like an endpoint may be the only thing telling an operator what a process is doing, or the only path by which the operator can intervene. Isolating an HMI mid-shift can leave operators blind. Isolating a controller can drop outputs. Isolation in OT is something you coordinate with operations, not something you do reflexively from a SOC console.

"Rotate the credentials"

Credential rotation is cheap and obvious in IT. In OT, an engineering account on a controller may be hard-coded into other systems, may be the only credential capable of safely shutting the process down, or may not be rotatable without a vendor on the call. Some controller credentials are stored physically on the device or in project files; some don't support rotation at all. Coordination first; action second.

Incident — NotPetya, 2017

The most cited example of IT-side action affecting OT availability is NotPetya. The wiper itself targeted IT systems, but its propagation reached the corporate networks of Maersk, Merck, Mondelez, Reckitt Benckiser and others, taking down systems that operations depended on — port logistics, manufacturing planning, production scheduling. Maersk reportedly lost the ability to dispatch ships and trucks for weeks; Merck's manufacturing of certain vaccines was disrupted. The lesson: OT availability often depends on IT systems whose criticality isn't obvious until they're gone.

What that means in practice

For an IT-trained practitioner moving into OT or working alongside OT engineers, three habits to develop:

  • Ask before acting, especially on anything connected to a control system, a historian, or an engineering workstation. The engineer's day-job is to know what the process is doing right now. Yours probably isn't.
  • Assume the system is fragile, even when it looks like a familiar Windows host. The host may be running engineering software that hasn't seen an OS reboot in 400 days for good reason.
  • Treat safety as a categorical override, not a value to be weighed against others. There is no efficiency, compliance, or security benefit that outweighs a credible safety risk.
Section 02

The Purdue model

The Purdue Enterprise Reference Architecture — usually just "Purdue" — is the dominant mental model for how OT networks are organised. It's a layered diagram, levels 0 through 5, with rougher access controls between adjacent levels and tighter controls across the IT/OT boundary that sits between levels 3 and 4. Every framework that touches OT either uses Purdue or maps to it.

Two things to know up front. First, Purdue is a reference architecture, not a blueprint. Real networks rarely match it cleanly — there are always shortcuts, legacy paths, and undocumented connections. Second, in modern environments the boundaries between levels are increasingly blurry: cloud historians, MES platforms in SaaS, IIoT sensors bypassing the hierarchy entirely. Treat Purdue as the model you start from, then verify what's actually there.

The levels

L5
Enterprise Email, ERP, corporate IT
The corporate IT estate. Email, ERP, file shares, identity. This is where the IT security team lives day-to-day. From OT's perspective, "the internet."
L4
Business Planning & Logistics MES, planning, scheduling
Business systems that talk to OT — manufacturing execution, production scheduling, supply chain planning. Still IT-administered, but with operational coupling.
— IT / OT boundary — the DMZ sits here (Level 3.5) —
L3.5
Industrial DMZ Historians, jump hosts, AV servers
The buffer zone between IT and OT. Historians and patch servers commonly live here, plus jump hosts for engineering and vendor remote access. Bidirectional traffic is brokered through this layer — direct L4-to-L3 paths should not exist.
L3
Site Operations Engineering workstations, site historian
Site-wide operational systems — engineering workstations for programming controllers, site-level historian, OT identity and management. Generally Windows-heavy. First level that's clearly OT-owned.
L2
Area Supervisory Control HMIs, SCADA servers, alarm systems
Operator-facing supervisory systems. HMIs (the screens operators watch), SCADA servers that aggregate data from controllers, alarm management. Real-time but not deterministic.
L1
Basic Control PLCs, RTUs, controllers
The controllers themselves — PLCs, RTUs, dedicated control hardware. Deterministic real-time systems making the millisecond decisions that drive the physical process. Often legacy, often proprietary, often with limited logging.
L0
Physical Process Sensors, actuators, motors, valves
The actual physical equipment — sensors reading temperature, pressure, flow, level; actuators opening valves, starting motors, moving robots. This is the thing the rest of the stack exists to control.

The Safety Instrumented System sits separately

One of the most important things about Purdue is what's not on the diagram in the same lane: the Safety Instrumented System (SIS). The SIS is an independent layer of protection — usually with its own controllers, its own network, sometimes its own physical cabinet — that exists to take the process to a safe state when basic process control fails. It's not Level 1; it's a parallel safety layer with stricter requirements, separate certifications, and a fundamentally different role.

For an IT-trained reader: think of the SIS as the airbag. The Basic Process Control System (BPCS — the Purdue Level 1/2 stack) drives the car. The SIS only acts when something has already gone wrong. Confusing the two leads to bad decisions.

Where the IT/OT boundary actually is

Officially: between Level 3 and Level 4, with the DMZ sitting at Level 3.5 mediating traffic. In practice the boundary is fuzzier than that, and where exactly the line falls determines who owns what — incident response authority, change control authority, patching authority. Common patterns:

  • IT owns L4 and L5. Always.
  • OT owns L0, L1, L2. Almost always — these touch physical equipment.
  • L3 and the DMZ are contested. Engineering workstations, jump hosts, and historians sit here. IT typically manages the underlying OS; OT typically owns the applications and the decisions about when systems can be touched. Both sides need to be in the room for changes.
Incident — Stuxnet, 2010

Stuxnet is the canonical example of an attack that crossed the full Purdue stack. It propagated via removable media on engineering workstations (Level 3), modified Siemens Step 7 project files (Level 3), and ultimately reprogrammed the logic on PLCs controlling centrifuges (Level 1) — while sending falsified data back up to the HMI (Level 2) so operators saw normal readings. It demonstrated that air-gaps are porous, that controller logic itself is a target, and that visibility from the HMI cannot be assumed authentic. Most modern OT architecture decisions trace back, directly or indirectly, to lessons from Stuxnet.

Section 03

The vocabulary

The fastest way to spot an IT person in an OT meeting is hearing them say "the SCADA" or "the PLC server." The OT vocabulary is precise and unforgiving — the words mean different things and getting them right earns enough trust to ask the questions you actually need to ask.

PLC
Programmable Logic Controller. A specialised industrial computer that executes control logic — reading sensors, driving actuators, in deterministic real-time. Typical brands: Siemens (S7), Rockwell (Allen-Bradley ControlLogix), Schneider (Modicon), Mitsubishi, ABB. The dominant controller type in manufacturing and continuous process industries.
RTU
Remote Terminal Unit. A controller hardened for remote locations and designed to communicate over slower or less reliable links — typical in electricity transmission, water utilities, oil and gas pipelines. Functionally similar to a PLC but with telemetry and protocol differences. The line between RTU and PLC has blurred over time.
IED
Intelligent Electronic Device. Used specifically in electric power. Protective relays, meters, and other devices that combine measurement, protection, and communications in one unit. If someone says "IED" in a substation context they mean this, not the other thing.
HMI
Human-Machine Interface. The screen the operator looks at to see process state and the touchscreen or console they use to issue commands. Almost always a Windows machine running vendor software (Wonderware/AVEVA, Rockwell FactoryTalk, Siemens WinCC, Ignition). Loss of HMI = loss of operator visibility = often a safety event.
EWS
Engineering Workstation. The machine an engineer uses to program and configure PLCs and other controllers. Holds the engineering software (Siemens TIA Portal, Rockwell Studio 5000, etc.) and the project files. High-value attacker target — compromise of the EWS gives access to the logic running on the controllers it manages.
SCADA
Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition. Not a thing — an architecture. A SCADA system is the combination of HMI, historian, controllers, and communications that lets operators supervise a geographically distributed process (a water network, an electricity grid, a pipeline). When someone says "the SCADA system" they usually mean the operator-facing aggregation layer (Level 2/3 in Purdue).
DCS
Distributed Control System. Like SCADA but for a continuous process at a single site — refineries, chemical plants, power generation. Highly integrated, often single-vendor (Emerson DeltaV, Honeywell Experion, Yokogawa CENTUM, Siemens PCS 7). The line between DCS and SCADA has blurred over time; the words are often used loosely.
BPCS
Basic Process Control System. The everyday control stack — controllers, HMI, historian, supervisory layer — that runs the process under normal conditions. Distinguished from SIS, which only intervenes when BPCS fails. Don't confuse the two.
SIS
Safety Instrumented System. An independent layer of automated safety protection. Separate controllers, often a separate network, designed and certified to take the process to a safe state when defined trip conditions are exceeded. Governed by IEC 61508 (general functional safety) and IEC 61511 (process industry). Highest-sensitivity asset class in OT.
Historian
Process Historian / Data Server. A specialised time-series database that records process values from controllers — temperatures, pressures, flow rates, valve positions — at high resolution over long periods. Common products: AVEVA PI System (formerly OSIsoft), GE Proficy, Wonderware Historian. Often the IT/OT bridge asset because it serves both control room dashboards and corporate reporting.
IACS
Industrial Automation and Control Systems. The umbrella term used by IEC 62443 to describe the whole OT estate — controllers, networks, HMIs, safety systems, the lot. When a document says "IACS" it means OT, more or less.
MES
Manufacturing Execution System. The Level-4 business application that translates production orders into work for the OT layer below — recipes, batch records, scheduling, traceability. Often the system that introduces IT/OT connectivity (and risk).
Jump host
Bastion / Jump Server. A controlled gateway through which engineers and vendors access OT systems. Usually sits in the DMZ (Level 3.5). Provides session control, recording, and an audit trail. Vendor remote access via jump host is the highest-risk attack vector in many OT environments precisely because it's an authorised path with elevated privileges.
Data diode
Unidirectional gateway. Hardware that physically allows data to flow in one direction only — typically OT to IT for monitoring data. Used to give business systems read access to OT data without any return path that could be exploited. Common in nuclear, defence, and high-criticality CNI deployments.
Section 04

The protocols

OT runs on a small set of protocols designed in eras when network security wasn't a design consideration. The practitioner-level point isn't deep protocol analysis — it's recognising which ones you'll see, what they're for, and what their security properties are. The honest summary: most of them have none, by design.

Protocol Used for Era / where seen Security properties
Modbus TCP / RTU Reading/writing registers on controllers. The lingua franca of industrial automation. Released 1979. Everywhere — manufacturing, building HVAC, water, smaller utilities. Modbus TCP rides on port 502. None. No authentication, no encryption. Anyone on the network can issue any command. Modbus Security (RFC) exists but is rarely deployed.
DNP3 Serial / IP Telemetry and supervisory control over WAN/serial. Designed for resilience over poor links. 1990s. Electric utilities and water in North America especially. UK uses are mixed; IEC 60870-5 more common. Secure Authentication (DNP3-SA) exists and is increasingly deployed. Without it: no authentication, no encryption.
IEC 60870-5-104 IP Telemetry and control between control centres and substations. The European equivalent of DNP3. Standardised 2000. UK electricity transmission and distribution. Port 2404. IEC 62351 defines TLS-based security overlays but adoption is uneven.
IEC 61850 Ethernet / IP Substation automation. Bus-style communication between IEDs (protective relays etc.) within a substation. 2000s onwards. Modern electrical substations. GOOSE messages are layer-2 and time-critical (millisecond latency requirements). IEC 62351-6 defines authentication for GOOSE; deployment is uneven. Time-criticality makes adding cryptographic overhead difficult.
EtherNet/IP Ethernet / IP Industrial Ethernet protocol used by Rockwell (Allen-Bradley) controllers. CIP (Common Industrial Protocol) over Ethernet. 1990s onwards. Manufacturing, especially North America. Common in any Rockwell-heavy estate. CIP Security exists (CIP Safety and CIP Security extensions) but adoption is limited to newer equipment.
PROFINET Ethernet Industrial Ethernet protocol used by Siemens controllers. Real-time variants for motion control. 2000s onwards. Manufacturing, especially Europe. Common in any Siemens-heavy estate. PROFINET Security Class 1/2/3 specifications exist but are recent and adoption is early.
OPC UA IP Modern protocol for exchanging data between OT systems and between OT and IT. The intended successor to OPC Classic (DCOM-based and a long-standing headache). 2008 onwards. Newer integrations, IT/OT bridges, cloud historians. The protocol vendors are betting on for the next decade. Designed with security as a first-class concern. Native authentication, encryption, and signing. Implementation matters — many deployments run OPC UA with security disabled.
MQTT IP Lightweight publish-subscribe protocol. Used in IIoT and cloud-bridge scenarios more than core OT. 2010s onwards. IIoT, smart-building, edge gateways. TLS support; payload encryption depends on implementation. Authentication via username/password or certificate.

What this means in practice

Three things to internalise from this table:

  • Most OT protocols offer no native security. Authentication, encryption, integrity — all bolted on, where they exist at all. The security model rests on network segmentation and physical access control, not on the protocol itself.
  • Active scanning is dangerous. Many controllers and protocol stacks crash on unexpected traffic. Nmap against a legacy PLC has a non-trivial probability of dropping it. Passive observation (SPAN ports, TAPs) is the standard approach for OT visibility tools precisely because of this.
  • "Air-gapped" usually isn't. Modbus, EtherNet/IP, PROFINET, OPC UA are all IP-based and travel over the same Ethernet that carries everything else. An "air-gap" between OT and IT often turns out to be one firewall rule, one engineering laptop, or one vendor jump host. Assume connectivity until proven otherwise.
Practitioner tip — protocol identification

For incident triage purposes, knowing the port number gets you most of the way. Port 502 on the wire = Modbus. Port 2404 = IEC 60870-5-104. Port 44818 + 2222 = EtherNet/IP. Port 102 = S7 (Siemens). Port 47808 (UDP) = BACnet (building automation). Unfamiliar protocols on the OT segment are not necessarily malicious — but they're worth identifying before anything else.

Section 05

Architecture patterns

The patterns you'll meet in nearly every OT environment, why they exist, and what tends to break them. Most OT security incidents trace to one of these patterns being implemented badly or worked around informally.

The IT/OT DMZ

The standard pattern for separating OT from IT is a DMZ — Level 3.5 in Purdue terms — that sits between the two and brokers all traffic. No direct connections between the IT estate (L4+) and the OT estate (L3 and below). Anything that needs to cross does so by terminating in the DMZ first.

Common DMZ residents: historian replicas (so business systems can read process data without reaching into OT), patch servers, anti-virus management, jump hosts, and any application that genuinely needs to span both sides (often MES). The discipline is that nothing initiates a connection across the DMZ — connections terminate on one side and a separate connection picks up on the other.

The way DMZs typically degrade: someone needs "just a quick" direct connection from a corporate analytics tool to a historian, or from a vendor support system to a controller. Those exceptions accumulate. Within a few years the DMZ exists on paper but the actual network has a dozen direct paths through it.

Data diodes — when the DMZ isn't enough

For higher-criticality environments, a software DMZ allowing controlled bidirectional traffic isn't enough. The answer is a data diode — a hardware appliance that physically permits traffic in only one direction (typically OT to IT). Light or electrical signals are designed so that no return path exists at the physical layer.

Common deployments: nuclear, defence, electricity transmission, some water utilities. The trade-off is real — no return path means no remote management of the OT side, no patch deployment, no incident response actions pushed in. That's a feature, not a bug, in environments where the cost of a return path being exploited is catastrophic.

Jump hosts and the vendor remote access problem

Vendor remote access is the single highest-risk attack vector in many OT environments. Vendors need it (they can't physically attend every site for every issue), it carries elevated privileges (vendors are often domain admins on the OT side), and it sits across the IT/OT boundary by design.

The pattern that works: jump hosts in the DMZ, with vendor sessions terminated and brokered through them. Session recording, MFA, time-windowed access tied to change tickets, named vendor engineers (not shared accounts), and monitoring of what happens in the session. The pattern that doesn't work: persistent VPN tunnels into OT segments, shared "vendor support" accounts with permanent credentials, and a culture of "the vendor is always allowed in."

The historian as IT/OT bridge

Historians are uniquely architectural because they're both an OT asset (they capture process data from controllers) and an IT-serving asset (they feed dashboards, reports, BI tools, and increasingly cloud platforms). They sit on the boundary by job description.

The healthy pattern: historian collection lives in OT; a read-only replica or aggregator lives in the DMZ; IT-side systems only ever talk to the replica. The unhealthy pattern: a single historian in OT that IT systems connect to directly, requiring inbound firewall holes from IT into OT. This is one of the most common ways a "segmented" OT network turns out, on inspection, not to be segmented.

Engineering workstations and project files

The engineering workstation is where controller logic is written, compiled, and downloaded to PLCs. The project files on it represent the actual control logic of the plant — high-value both as attacker targets and as engineering artefacts. Compromise of an EWS gives an attacker the ability to push modified logic to controllers; loss of project files makes future engineering changes much harder.

Healthy practice: EWS hosts are dedicated, hardened, kept off the wider corporate network, and project files are version-controlled with separate authentication. In reality: many EWS are dual-purpose machines also used for email, web browsing, and general engineering work — which is one reason phishing-borne malware so often ends up with a foothold adjacent to OT.

Remote operations and the cloud question

Increasing pressure to extend OT monitoring and even control to cloud platforms — for fleet visibility across sites, for predictive maintenance, for vendor-managed services. This is the next-decade question for OT architecture, and different sectors are answering it differently. Water and electricity utilities are cautious; manufacturing is further along; building management is essentially there already.

The risk pattern: cloud connectivity introduced for monitoring use cases ("read-only, no control") quietly becomes a control surface as features expand. The architectural discipline — keep it strictly read-only, terminate on the OT side with a data diode or equivalent — is harder to maintain over time than to define on day one.

Incident — Colonial Pipeline, 2021

Colonial Pipeline shut down their pipeline operations after a ransomware attack on their IT systems. The attackers reached the IT estate via a compromised VPN credential; they did not, according to public reporting, reach the OT systems controlling the pipeline. Operations were halted by Colonial themselves, partly because billing systems (IT) were unavailable and partly out of caution about whether OT systems might also be at risk. The lesson: IT compromise can force an OT shutdown even when OT itself is technically untouched, when shared dependencies span both sides. Architecture that minimises those shared dependencies — billing, identity, monitoring, comms — is part of OT resilience, not just IT hygiene.

Section 06

Standards & frameworks

Five frameworks worth knowing, in roughly the order a UK practitioner is likely to meet them. Each gets a one-paragraph orientation here — for actual content, go to the official source. The point is to know which one to reach for and how they relate to each other, not to summarise content that already exists in better form elsewhere.

UK Regulatory
NCSC CAF v4
The Cyber Assessment Framework. The UK's regulatory assessment framework for organisations covered by NIS Regulations — Operators of Essential Services and Relevant Digital Service Providers. Outcome-based: four objectives, fourteen principles, forty-one contributing outcomes. Sector regulators (Ofgem, Ofwat, etc.) use CAF to assess CNI compliance. If you're in UK CNI, this is the framework regulators score you against.
When to reach for it Regulatory assessment, NIS reporting, board-level resilience reporting, structuring a UK CNI security programme.
ncsc.gov.uk/cyber-assessment-framework →
International Technical Standard
IEC 62443
The international standard family for the security of Industrial Automation and Control Systems (IACS). Multiple parts covering programmes (62443-2-1), system requirements (62443-3-3), component requirements (62443-4-2). Built around zones, conduits, and Security Levels SL-1 through SL-4. Widely adopted by industrial equipment vendors and integrators. In the UK CNI context, IEC 62443 sits alongside CAF — it's the technical standard used to implement the OT security that CAF expects in principle.
When to reach for it Designing an OT security architecture, specifying requirements to vendors, formal SL assessment, working with engineering teams who already use it.
isa.org/standards/62443 →
US Government Guidance
NIST SP 800-82r3
Guide to Operational Technology Security. The detailed, practitioner-oriented reference document for OT security from the US National Institute of Standards and Technology. Revision 3, published 2023, broadened scope from ICS to OT generally — now covers building automation, transportation, medical devices alongside traditional industrial control. Free to download. Around 300 pages. The single best operational reference if you're going to read one document end-to-end.
When to reach for it Self-education, security control selection, detailed operational guidance, mapping to other frameworks.
csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/82/r3 →
UK Government Guidance
NCSC OT Guidance
The NCSC's targeted guidance for OT environments — principles, sector-specific notes, and supporting material that aligns with CAF. Lighter than NIST SP 800-82r3 but better calibrated to UK regulatory and sector context. Includes specific guidance on secure remote access, OT-aware patching, and SIS-related considerations.
When to reach for it UK-specific implementation guidance, briefing senior stakeholders, sector context for CNI conversations.
ncsc.gov.uk/operational-technology →
Functional Safety
IEC 61508 / IEC 61511
Functional safety standards. IEC 61508 is the general standard for safety-related systems across industries. IEC 61511 is the process-industry-specific application of 61508, covering Safety Instrumented Systems in chemical, oil & gas, and similar plants. Not security standards — but anything that could affect a SIS is governed by these, and the security/safety boundary is where many OT decisions live. If your environment has a SIS, these standards govern its design and any change to it.
When to reach for it Anything touching a Safety Instrumented System, working with functional safety engineers, change control on safety-related systems.
iec.ch/functional-safety →

How they relate

For a UK practitioner, a useful mental model: CAF is what the regulator assesses you against; IEC 62443 is what your engineering teams and integrators build to; NIST 800-82r3 is the encyclopaedic reference you go to for depth; NCSC OT guidance is the UK-flavoured practitioner material; IEC 61508/61511 govern anything that touches safety. They don't conflict in any meaningful way; they cover different layers of the same problem.

UK regulatory direction of travel

The NIS Regulations 2018 currently provide the UK regulatory framework for CNI cyber. The forthcoming Cyber Security and Resilience Bill is expected to broaden coverage and align more closely with the EU's NIS2 Directive. CAF v4 (August 2025) was updated in anticipation. Sector regulators continue to set sector-specific expectations on top — Ofgem for energy, Ofwat for water, the ORR for rail, HSE for safety-related plant. None of this changes the underlying technical standards (62443, 61508/61511), but it does change reporting obligations and assessment frequency.

Incident — Triton / Trisis, 2017

Triton (also Trisis or HatMan) was malware that specifically targeted Schneider Electric Triconex safety controllers at a petrochemical plant in Saudi Arabia. The attackers reached the safety controllers — successfully — and were attempting to modify their logic when they were detected, having accidentally tripped the SIS into a safe-state shutdown. Had they succeeded, the safety layer protecting the plant from physical disaster would have been silently disabled. Triton is the case study for why SIS deserves separate treatment from BPCS, why IEC 61508/61511 govern these systems, and why "is this connected to a safety system" is the single most important question to answer when triaging an OT incident.

Section 07

Safety & where to go next

The throughline of this document has been "OT is different because safety matters in a way IT rarely has to think about." This last section closes the loop on the safety side, then points at the next steps for a practitioner who wants to go deeper.

The safety / security distinction

The traditional engineering definitions: safety is freedom from unacceptable risk of physical injury or damage; security is freedom from intentional unauthorised acts. They overlap heavily in OT — a security failure can cause a safety event, and a safety system failure can be exploited for security impact. But the disciplines are distinct in practice: safety is governed by functional safety standards (IEC 61508/61511), validated by safety engineers, and certified to specific safety integrity levels (SILs). Security is governed by IEC 62443 and similar, validated by security engineers, against security levels (SLs). Same acronym shape, very different things.

What an IT-trained responder must never do

Three categorical rules that hold regardless of how the rest of an incident is going:

  • Do not take action on a Safety Instrumented System. No probing, no scanning, no isolation, no patching, no credential rotation. SIS-related events are major incidents requiring engineering, operations, and safety personnel by default. Even passive monitoring of SIS network traffic warrants careful coordination before it starts.
  • Do not assume "the safety system will catch it." The SIS exists to protect against process-side failures, not necessarily against deliberately-engineered attacks against itself. Triton showed the SIS itself is reachable. A compromised SIS protects nothing.
  • Do not make decisions about safety-related systems without engineering authority on the line. The IT chain of command does not extend to safety. Decisions about availability, isolation, or any change to a safety-related system require engineering and (usually) safety sign-off — not the IR Lead alone, not the SOC, not the on-call.

Training paths worth the time

Two genuinely well-regarded training paths if you want a formal credential and the structured learning that goes with it:

  • SANS ICS courses (ICS410 introductory, ICS515 incident response, ICS612 advanced) — the most respected practitioner training in the space. Expensive, intensive, well-taught.
  • ISA/IEC 62443 certification path — vendor-neutral certification tied to the standard. More structured than SANS, less practitioner-skills-focused. Useful for engineering-adjacent roles or where the standard itself is the working framework.

One last thing

The most common mistake an IT-trained person makes in OT is acting confidently. The OT engineer's working assumption is that the system is fragile, that changes have consequences, and that the right pace is "slow enough to be sure." The faster you adopt that disposition, the faster you'll be useful. The IT instinct to move fast, fix things, and apologise later doesn't work here, and the people you'll be working with already know that. Match their pace and the trust follows.
